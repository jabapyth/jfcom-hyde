Creative Education

Posted: Thu, 28 Mar 2013
  • education
  • reform

It’s time to stop ignoring the need for general education reform. It’s true, there are some of us who happen to be good at the kinds of things that standardized tests are looking for, and who learn to love to learn despite their public education. But for every one who survives public schooling with creativity in tact, there are a dozen others who buy in to the game, who either spend their time trying to be good at the things that are tested, or sacrifice their creativity and lose all desire to learn because the school system so often incentivises conformity and punishes their creativity.

The tragedy here is that these people spend the first few decades of their lives “preparing for the workforce”, hoping to gain an education so they will be successful in providing for themselves, only to find that the attributes that are critical for occupational success are the very characteristics that public school had beaten out of them.

Assuming that the goal is to educate everyone as much as possible, to increase their intelligence and help them have more opportunities throughout their lives, the current system of “education” has more than a few curiosities. Why, for example, do high schools around the country spend a large amount of time “teaching to” the SAT, the ACT, and various other standardized tests? Closer inspection reveals that much of “education” is geared toward ranking children in addition to—and sometimes in the place of—teaching them.

The need for ranking America’s youth follows naturally from the notion that there are a limited number of jobs and a large number of potential employees. Employers naturally would want to select the best and “most intelligent” for the best jobs. This idea was born of the industrial revolution, where there was indeed a tremendous glut of labor as people migrated to urban centers en masse. In such a world of job scarcity, students would in fact want to be ranked so that they can have the best job possible. This scenario, however, is a delusion.

I hope none of us believe in a world where, regardless of how educated we all are, the lowest ranked will just have to work at McDonalds, because there just won’t be enough good, fulfilling jobs for everyone. It’s buy-in to this fallacy of scarcity that leads to the type of ranking—not schooling—which we see happening to our children. With school reform that encourages creativity and innovation, we will see many more entrepreneurs pushing the limits of human ability which will create more jobs and lead to a more flourishing economy overall.
